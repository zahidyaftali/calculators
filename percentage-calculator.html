What is Percentage?

A percentage is a way of expressing a number as a fraction of 100. The word "percent" comes from the Latin per centum, meaning "by the hundred." Percentages are widely used in everyday life — from exam scores and tax rates to discounts and financial returns.

In academic contexts, percentage is the most universally understood metric for comparing performance. Whether you scored 450 out of 500 or 270 out of 300, converting to percentage allows fair comparison across different total marks systems.

Percentage Formula

Percentage = (Obtained Marks ÷ Total Marks) × 100

Example: (450 ÷ 500) × 100 = 90%

Grade Classification System

Most educational systems use standard grade bands based on percentage:

  • 90% and above — A+ Grade (Excellent / Distinction)
  • 80% – 89% — A Grade (Very Good / First Class)
  • 70% – 79% — B Grade (Good / Second Class)
  • 60% – 69% — C Grade (Average / Third Class)
  • 50% – 59% — D Grade (Below Average / Pass)
  • Below 50% — F Grade (Fail)

Note: Grade boundaries vary by institution, university, or examination board. The above is a common standard.

Other Percentage Calculations

Percentages are used in many contexts beyond marks:

  • Percentage increase: [(New Value – Old Value) / Old Value] × 100
  • Percentage decrease: [(Old Value – New Value) / Old Value] × 100
  • What % is X of Y: (X / Y) × 100
  • X% of Y: (X / 100) × Y

Why Percentages Matter in Academics

Academic percentages are used for:

  • University admissions cutoffs and merit lists
  • Scholarship eligibility (often requiring 75% or above)
  • Job applications where minimum percentage criteria are specified
  • Professional exam eligibility (CA, law, medical boards)
  • CGPA conversion and equivalency assessments

CGPA to Percentage Conversion

Many universities now use CGPA (Cumulative Grade Point Average) instead of direct percentage. Common conversion formulas:

CBSE formula: Percentage = CGPA × 9.5
Anna University: Percentage = (CGPA – 0.5) × 10
Mumbai University: Percentage = (CGPA × 7.1) + 11

Always refer to your specific institution's official conversion formula.
