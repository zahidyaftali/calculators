What is BMI?

Body Mass Index (BMI) is a simple numerical measure of a person's body fat based on their weight and height. It is widely used by doctors, dietitians, and health professionals as a quick screening tool to identify potential weight problems in adults. While BMI does not directly measure body fat, it correlates well with direct measures of body fat and can indicate health risks.

Developed by Belgian mathematician Adolphe Quetelet in the 19th century, BMI has become the most widely accepted international standard for assessing healthy body weight relative to height. Organizations like the World Health Organization (WHO) and the Centers for Disease Control (CDC) use BMI classifications to monitor population health.

How is BMI Calculated?

The BMI formula is straightforward:

BMI = Weight (kg) ÷ [Height (m)]²

Or equivalently: BMI = Weight (kg) ÷ [(Height in cm ÷ 100)²]

For example, if you weigh 70 kg and are 175 cm (1.75 m) tall: BMI = 70 ÷ (1.75)² = 70 ÷ 3.0625 = 22.86 — which falls in the Normal range.

BMI Categories (WHO Standard)

  • Below 18.5 — Underweight: May indicate malnutrition or an eating disorder.
  • 18.5 – 24.9 — Normal Weight: Healthy range associated with lower health risks.
  • 25.0 – 29.9 — Overweight: Slightly elevated risk for conditions like heart disease and diabetes.
  • 30.0 – 34.9 — Obese (Class I): Significant increased risk for metabolic diseases.
  • 35.0 – 39.9 — Obese (Class II): High health risk category.
  • 40.0 and above — Obese (Class III / Morbid Obesity): Very high risk; medical intervention recommended.

Limitations of BMI

While BMI is a useful population-level screening tool, it has some limitations for individuals:

  • It does not distinguish between muscle mass and fat mass — athletes may have high BMI despite low body fat.
  • It may underestimate fat in older adults who have lost muscle mass.
  • BMI may differ in accuracy across different ethnicities.
  • It does not account for fat distribution (e.g., visceral vs. subcutaneous fat).

Always consult a healthcare professional for a comprehensive health assessment. BMI is one of many tools, not a definitive diagnosis.

How to Achieve a Healthy BMI

If your BMI is outside the normal range, lifestyle changes can help:

  • Balanced diet: Focus on whole foods, lean proteins, vegetables, and whole grains.
  • Regular exercise: Aim for at least 150 minutes of moderate activity per week.
  • Adequate sleep: Poor sleep is linked to weight gain and hormonal imbalances.
  • Stress management: Chronic stress can contribute to emotional eating and weight gain.
  • Professional guidance: Work with a doctor or dietitian for a personalized plan.
