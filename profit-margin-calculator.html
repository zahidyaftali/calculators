What is Profit Margin?

Profit margin is a financial metric that measures the percentage of revenue that a business retains as profit after accounting for costs. It is one of the most important indicators of a business's financial health and operational efficiency.

A higher profit margin means a company keeps more money for every rupee of sales, while a low margin suggests high costs or intense price competition. Businesses use profit margins to compare performance over time, benchmark against industry averages, and make pricing decisions.

Profit Margin Formula

Profit Margin (%) = [(Selling Price − Cost Price) ÷ Selling Price] × 100

Example: Cost = ₹500, Selling = ₹750
Profit = ₹250
Profit Margin = (250 ÷ 750) × 100 = 33.33%

Profit Margin vs. Markup – Key Difference

These terms are often confused but represent very different things:

Profit Margin = (Profit ÷ Selling Price) × 100 ← based on selling price
Markup = (Profit ÷ Cost Price) × 100 ← based on cost price
  • If Cost = ₹100 and Selling = ₹150: Profit = ₹50
  • Profit Margin = 50/150 × 100 = 33.33%
  • Markup = 50/100 × 100 = 50%

Markup is always higher than the corresponding profit margin for the same transaction. Retailers typically think in markup terms when pricing, but margins are used when reporting financial performance.

Types of Profit Margin

  • Gross Profit Margin: Revenue minus cost of goods sold (COGS). Measures production efficiency.
  • Operating Profit Margin: Subtracts operating expenses. Measures operational efficiency.
  • Net Profit Margin: After all deductions including taxes and interest. The true bottom line.

Industry Profit Margin Benchmarks

  • Retail (FMCG): 2% – 8% net margin is typical
  • Software / SaaS: 20% – 40% net margin is common
  • Restaurant / Food: 3% – 9% is industry average
  • E-commerce: 2% – 5% due to high logistics costs
  • Luxury goods: 50% – 70% gross margins are standard

How to Improve Your Profit Margin

  • Reduce COGS through better supplier negotiations or bulk purchasing
  • Increase selling price gradually while maintaining customer satisfaction
  • Cut operational overhead — rent, utilities, unnecessary subscriptions
  • Focus on high-margin products and reduce low-margin SKUs
  • Improve conversion rates to spread fixed costs over more units
